## Expecting the Unexpected

No plan survives contact with the enemy unchanged. As an FTL, your ability to adapt quickly separates good leaders from struggling ones.

## Common Contingencies

### Loss of a Team Member (Casualty)

**What happens:** One of your people goes down.

**Immediate actions:**

1. Suppress the threat (everyone returns fire)
2. Get the casualty to cover
3. Continue the mission with 5 people instead of 6

**Continuing operations:**

- The buddy pair of the casualty now has 1 person, they need to be paired with another buddy pair
- You're down 16% of your firepower
- Adjust your positioning: maybe pull a team back for recovery or consolidation
- Be more conservative in aggressive actions
- Consider requesting reinforcement if the mission is complex

### Loss of a Specialist (MAT, AR, Medic, etc.)

**What happens:** The person with a critical role goes down.

**Immediate actions:**

1. Same as any casualty—suppress and recover
2. Quickly reassess who can take over the role

**Adapting to lost specialty:**

- **No medic?** Other members of your team may be medic trained, they can take over the role.
- **No AR?** You lose volume of fire. Rely more on positioning and suppression. Be less aggressive.
- **No MAT?** You lose anti-tank capability. Plan accordingly if enemy vehicles are a possibility.
- **No marksman or other specialist?** Whatever they were carrying becomes less available. Plan accordingly.

**Example:** Your medic goes down. You can still treat basic wounds, but urgent casualties might not survive. This changes your risk calculus—you can't afford more people getting hit.

### Comms Failure

**What happens:** Your radio breaks, jams, or someone can't reach you.

**Immediate assessment:**

- Can you still hear your OL? (One-way comms is better than none)
- Can you communicate with your color teams? (Usually yes, shorter range)
- Is this temporary or permanent?

**Operating with limited comms:**

- If you can't hear OL: Continue the mission to the best of your understanding. They'll try to reach you. Proceed with caution.
- If color teams can't reach you: Move to where they can see you. Use hand signals. Slow down operations.
- If you have local comms only: Operate independently. Avoid coordinating with other teams. Focus on your immediate objective.

**Planning for radio loss:**

Radios can be blocked by large terrain features, buildings, or jamming. Always have a backup plan for communication:

- Have a time limit for objectives, teams pull out and return to a rally point or spot with known comms if they lose contact
- Use vehicles as relays if possible

### Plan Changes (Objective Changes Mid-Mission)

**What happens:** OL calls and says the plan has changed.

**Immediate actions:**

1. Confirm you understand the new objective
2. Repeat it back to OL
3. Ask clarifying questions if needed
4. Brief your color teams on the change

**Adapting to new objectives:**

- **Shift positioning if needed.** Where you are now might not be good for the new objective.
- **Consider resource availability.** Do you have enough ammo and energy for the new objective?
- **Adjust speed and risk.** New objectives have different requirements.

**Example:** You're moving to establish a checkpoint when OL says "Scratch that, we need you to pursue the convoy that just left town." New objective, completely different focus. You turn and move to intercept instead.

### Ambush: You're Pinned

**What happens:** Enemy attacks and your team is pinned or taking heavy casualties.

**First 10 seconds:**

1. Return fire immediately
2. Get people to cover
3. Identify threat location and strength

**Assessment (10-30 seconds):**

- Where is the fire coming from?
- How many enemy?
- Can you maneuver or are you pinned?
- Do you have cover?

**Decision (30+ seconds):**

- **Can you assault?** If enemy is pinned and you have numbers, attack.
- **Can you maneuver around?** If one team can flank, do it.
- **Can you suppress and extract?** If you're in a bad position, break contact and move.
- **Do you need support?** If you can't win this alone, call OL.

**Example:** Blue is ambushed from a ridge line. They take cover and return fire. Green moves to flank the ridge line. Red provides suppressive fire from their position. Blue pops up and assaults while Green is flanking. Ambush is broken.

### Friendly Fire Incident

**What happens:** Your team fires on another friendly team or vice versa.

**Immediate actions:**

1. **Cease fire.** Stop shooting right now.
2. **Identify what happened.** Who fired? At whom?
3. **Establish communication** with the other team or OL
4. **Confirm positions** to prevent it happening again

**Recovery:**

- Call OL and report what happened
- Get accountability (did anyone get hit? How bad?)
- Reposition if needed to clear confusion
- Brief all color teams on positions to prevent recurrence

**Prevention (your responsibility as FTL):**

- Maintain radio contact with other teams
- Know where friendlies are positioned
- Clearly identify targets before opening fire
- Brief your color teams on friendly positions

**Example:** Your Blue team is attacking a building from the south. Another FTL's Green team moves to cut off enemy escape to the east. You don't coordinate or update your Blue on their position. Blue starts engaging before you stop them. Thankfully no one is hit, but it was close. You immediately radio the other team's position to Blue and coordinate so it doesn't happen again.

### Unexpected Enemy Contact

**What happens:** You contact enemy in a place you didn't expect them.

**Decision tree:**

1. **Can you suppress and avoid them?** If they're not blocking you, suppress and move around.
2. **Can you suppress and push through?** If they're a light force, engage and advance.
3. **Do you need to pull back?** If they're stronger than expected, fall back and call for support.
4. **Is this a different enemy than expected?** Report to OL immediately.

**Adapting your mission:**

- If they're a minor obstacle: Adjust your route or timing, continue.
- If they're significant: You might need to change your approach entirely.
- If they're a major force: Might need to abort the original mission and handle this.

**Example:** You're supposed to move through a valley to reach an objective. You find a fortified enemy position in the valley. Change of plans: you move around the valley using higher terrain, taking longer but avoiding the position.

## Principle of Adaptation: "Good Enough and Fast"

The key to adaptation is making a decision quickly and moving forward:

- **Perfect decision after 5 minutes** = too late, situation has changed
- **Good decision in 30 seconds** = gets things moving in the right direction
- **Adapt again if needed** = first decision doesn't have to be perfect

Your team looks to you for direction. Indecision is worse than imperfect direction.

## Communicating Changes to Your Team

When you adapt the plan, tell your team clearly:

"New plan. We're moving north instead of east because of that contact. Green still covers the right flank, Blue is with me now."

Notice this:

- **States what's changing** (moving north not east)
- **Explains why** (because of contact)
- **Clarifies roles** (Green still covers right flank, Blue is with FTL)
- **Invites questions** (in case someone is confused)
- **Moves forward** (doesn't dwell on the change)

## Building Adaptability into Your Team

You can't prevent all problems, but you can prepare your team:

- **Train in different scenarios.** Combat rarely goes to plan.
- **Practice improvisation.** What if your planned route is blocked? What if you lose a person?
- **Rehearse drills.** Casualty procedures, ambush response, position changes.
- **Build confidence.** Teams that have overcome obstacles before are less panicked by new ones.
- **Emphasize mission over plan.** The plan might change, but the mission doesn't. Focus on accomplishing the goal.

## Common Adaptation Mistakes

- **Freezing up.** Situation changes and you don't respond. Green and Blue are confused.
- **Overreacting.** Small contact happens and you pull back when you should push forward.
- **Not informing OL.** You adapt the plan but don't tell OL what you're doing. They make decisions based on old plan.
- **Changing too often.** You adapt every few minutes. Your color teams get whiplash and stop trusting your direction.
- **Not involving color teams.** You change the plan but don't communicate it clearly. Green and Blue don't understand the new approach.

## The Bottom Line

Plans rarely go perfectly. Your ability to stay calm, assess the situation, make a good-enough decision quickly, and adapt your team's actions separates effective FTLs from ineffective ones.

The best teams are the ones that have practiced adaptation so much that changing direction becomes natural, not shocking.
