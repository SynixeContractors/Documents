## Decentralized Decision-Making

As an FTL, your primary job is not to micromanage every action your team takes. Instead, you should provide clear **goals and constraints**, then trust your color teams to figure out how to achieve them.

This is called decentralized decision-making, and it's how effective small units operate.

### The FTL's Role

You set the "what" and the "why":

- What is the objective?
- Why are we doing this?
- What are the constraints? (Don't cross the river, don't go past the tree line, stay in radio contact)

Your color teams figure out the "how":

- How do we position ourselves?
- How do we approach?
- How do we maintain security while accomplishing the goal?

**Example:**

**Bad (Micromanagement):** "Green, move to the building at grid 123456. Stop behind the wall. Peek around the corner on my mark. Fire if you see anything."

**Good (Goal-based):** "Green, I want overwatch on that building. Make sure nothing can approach from the north. Report what you see."

In the first example, if the situation changes, Green is confused and can't adapt. In the second example, they understand the *goal* and can make decisions on their own.

## When to Make Decisions Independently

As an FTL, you should make decisions independently in most situations. You are equipped, trained, and trusted to do so. Your OL should not need to approve every action you take.

### Decisions You Should Make Without Asking OL

- Positioning and movement of your team
- How to use cover and terrain
- Spacing and formation
- Where to establish security
- How to accomplish your assigned objective
- Response to minor threats (enemy contact, ambush)
- Requesting support from your team's resources

**You are trusted to operate effectively.** Make the decisions you need to make.

### When to Consult Your OL

- Major changes to the mission or objective
- Situations that will significantly impact other teams
- When you don't understand the intent behind an order
- When you're unsure if an action aligns with the broader plan
- Major resource requests (air support, reinforcements, etc.)
- When your team cannot accomplish the task as given

## Communication Without Permission

A common mistake FTLs make is over-communicating minor decisions or waiting for approval on things they should decide. This clogs the radio and slows down operations.

**Instead:**

- **Inform, don't ask permission.** "Command, Blue is moving to the north ridge for overwatch" is better than "Command, can Blue move to the north ridge?"
- **Act first if necessary, report immediately after.** If you detect a threat and need to reposition, do it. Then inform OL: "Command, Blue is repositioning east due to activity. Moving now."
- **Keep it brief.** OL doesn't need a detailed explanation of how you positioned your color teams. They need to know your team's status and location.

## Handling Ambiguity

Sometimes orders are unclear, incomplete, or don't fully account for what's on the ground. In these situations, you must make a decision.

### When Orders Don't Make Sense

1. **Ask for clarification if you have time.** "Command, just to confirm—do you want us to clear the town or establish a perimeter?"
2. **Make a reasonable decision if you don't.** If you can't reach OL or the situation is developing too fast, interpret the order in a way that makes sense and accomplishes the overall goal.
3. **Inform OL after.** "Command, executed as follows... we determined X was the best course of action."

A decision that's good enough and made on time is better than perfect indecision.

### Making the Call Under Pressure

In contact or high-stress situations, you won't have time to think deeply. You need to:

1. **Know your objective.** If you understand what you're trying to accomplish, most decisions become obvious.
2. **Remember your constraints.** ROE, avoid civilian areas, maintain contact with other teams, etc.
3. **Act decisively.** Your team looks to you for confidence, even if you're uncertain.
4. **Adapt quickly.** If the first decision isn't working, make a new one.

## Balancing Speed and Caution

There's a constant tension between speed (acting fast) and caution (avoiding mistakes). The right balance depends on the situation.

- **High-risk situations:** Slow down. Take time to understand what's happening. Poor decisions in combat kill people.
- **Low-threat movements:** Move fast. Get to your objective. Don't overthink it.
- **Ambiguous situations:** Gather information before acting, but don't wait too long. Thirty seconds of reconnaissance can save you, but thirty minutes of planning wastes time.

**Trust your gut.** If something feels wrong, it usually is. If something feels right, move forward.

## Responsibility and Accountability

When you make a decision, you own the outcome—good or bad. That's part of being a leader.

- **Explain your thinking.** If something goes wrong, tell OL why you made the decision you did.
- **Learn and adjust.** After the mission, think about what you'd do differently.
- **Don't make excuses.** Explain what happened, not why it wasn't your fault.

Your team will trust you more if you own your decisions, both the good ones and the mistakes.

## Color Team Radio Communication

When addressing your color teams on the radio, use their color code. This keeps communications clear and ensures the right person is responding:

- **Yellow:** You (FTL)
- **Red:** Medic (often paired with the FTL)
- **Green:** Rifleman (primary radio operator) + Specialist
- **Blue:** Rifleman (primary radio operator) + Specialist

The first person listed in each pair is responsible for responding when that color is addressed.
