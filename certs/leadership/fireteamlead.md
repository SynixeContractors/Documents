## Overview

A fire team leader (FTL) is the lowest level of leadership in Synixe. They are usually responsible for a team of 6 members (3 buddy pairs), and are expected to be able to lead them effectively in combat. Their goal can range from "Move to that wall" to "Secure that town". They need to be ready to make decisions and operate as part of a large element, or independently.

**Standard Fire Team Composition:**

- **Yellow:** You (FTL)
- **Red:** Medic (paired with you)
- **Green:** Rifleman (radio rep)
- **Blue:** Rifleman (radio rep)

### The Core Philosophy: Lead by Objectives, Not Micromanagement

As an FTL, your job is to give your color teams (buddy pairs) clear **goals and constraints**, then trust them to figure out how to accomplish those goals. Don't tell them exactly where to stand or how to move.

**Bad:** "Green, move to the building at grid 123456, get behind the wall, watch the north side."

**Good:** "Green, I want eyes on the northern approach. Make sure nothing comes down that road. Let me know what you see."

The first approach doesn't allow your team to adapt. The second one gives them a mission and trusts them to execute it. This is how effective small units operate. See [Decision-Making & Autonomy](certs/leadership/decisions.md) and [Tactical Positioning](certs/leadership/tactics.md) for more on this approach.

## Gear Checks & Team Management

The FTL's role starts before the mission, by ensuring their team is prepared for the mission, and is ready to go.
They need to make sure their team:

- Is kit'd up appropriately for the mission at hand
- Knows the greater plan, and their team's role in it
- Is aware of the rules of engagement (ROE)
- Is on the correct radio channels
- Knows who their buddy is

During gear checks, everyone in the team should go over:

- Their role (Rifleman, Marksman, Autorifleman, Medic, etc)
- That they have all of the required gear for a contractor
- That they have all specialized gear
- They have enough ammunition, a bodybag, and a map, GPS, and compass
- They are on the correct radio channels, and appear on the map with radio GPS
- Any tools they have (LAT, Entrenching Tool, Grenadier, etc)

As the FTL, you should ensure you learn from the gear check:

- Who is in your team
- Who is a specialist
- Who has tools

## Information Sharing

Effective leadership relies on clear communication flowing both up and down the chain of command. Your information sharing practices directly impact your team's effectiveness and safety.

### From Operation Lead to Your Team

Your Operation Lead is responsible for providing you with critical information before and during the mission. They should ensure you understand:

- The mission objective and overall goal
- Your team's specific role and responsibilities
- The broader situation and enemy/threat disposition
- Any rules of engagement (ROE) or special restrictions
- Updates when the situation changes significantly

**Your responsibility:** Ensure your team understands all of this information. A team that knows why they're doing something and how it fits into the larger picture will be more effective and safer.

### From Your Team to the Operation Lead

Your OL needs to know what's happening on the ground. You must keep them informed of:

- Your team's location and status
- Enemy contact or threats you've discovered
- Casualties or equipment loss
- Any deviations from their orders or the plan
- Intelligence your team has gathered
- Resource shortages or problems that could affect the mission

Do not assume your OL knows what you're doing. If your team must deviate from orders, inform them immediately. If the situation changes, pass it up. A leader that operates without current information cannot make good decisions.

### Within Your Team

Your team needs to understand what's happening so they can be effective soldiers, not just order-takers:

- Make sure everyone knows the objective
- Brief them on what's expected of them specifically
- Keep them updated as the situation develops
- Ensure your buddy system is working — buddies need to stay aware of each other
- Encourage situational awareness; soldiers who understand the bigger picture catch things you might miss

## Leading Your Team

### Areas of Responsibility

In any operation, spatial organization is critical to prevent confusion, inefficiency, and dangerous incidents like blue-on-blue situations. We use the "Rule of 10" to define areas of responsibility:

**Buddy Pair:** Each pair of soldiers is responsible for the **10 meters** around them. They should always be aware of what's going on in this area, and not be constantly next to another buddy pair.

**Fire Team:** Your team of 6 soldiers (3 buddy pairs) is responsible for the **100 meters** around them. You should be aware of what's happening within this area, monitor for threats, and maintain security within this zone.

**Squad:** A squad-level element is responsible for the **1000 meters** (1 kilometer) around them. This is the larger operational area where squad leadership maintains situational awareness and coordinates with other teams.

#### Cross-Team Movement in Another Team's Area

When teams need to move through or operate in another team's area of responsibility, coordination is essential. Failing to coordinate can result in:

- Confusion about who is responsible for what
- Inefficient use of fire and positioning
- Blue-on-blue incidents where friendly forces target each other

**Always inform the team whose area you're operating in.** Give them:

- Your intended route and timing
- How long you'll be in their area
- Any support or coordination you need

The controlling team's leader can then position their people appropriately and avoid confusion. Communication prevents disaster.

### Movement

FTLs should set the order, speed, and direction of any movements their element needs to make. The order should be clear to everyone in the element, and use the buddy system. The FTL can be anywhere in the order, but being at the front provides the most effective leading with extra risk.

There are 6 different speeds of movement with weapons in Arma.

#### Weapon Raised

- **Combat Walk** <kbd>Ctrl + C</kbd>
- **Combat Jog** <kbd>C</kbd>

#### Weapon Lowered

- **Walk** <kbd>Ctrl + C</kbd>
- **Slow Jog** <kbd>CC</kbd>
- **Jog**
- **Sprint** <kbd>⇧ Shift</kbd>

Walking while crouched uses significantly more stamina than being upright. Use sparingly.

Handrails are useful terrain features that can aid with keeping on tracking during large movements. Handrails can be rivers, streets, cliffs, etc. Make mental notes to travel on the left or right, and usually at least 200m away. You can also pick out a mountain, tall building, etc. as a target to walk towards.

Avoid traveling through open areas, or utilize bounding when there is not an alternative. Use valleys, bushes, trees, or any other terrain features that provide your element cover, concealment, and an advantageous position over your adversary.
