## Learning from Mistakes

The best way to improve as an FTL is to learn from mistakes, both yours and others'. Here are the most common mistakes that get people killed or missions failed.

## Spacing and Clustering

**The Mistake:** Team members are too close together during movement or static positions.

**Why it's bad:**

- Single burst of fire wounds multiple people
- Casualties pile up fast
- Ambush kills half your team at once

**How to fix it:**

- Brief spacing requirements: buddy pairs 5-10m apart, teams 50-100m apart
- Actively manage spacing during movement
- Spread out, always—it's not about comfort, it's about survival

**Example:** You're moving to a building when an ambush happens. All three people in a line are hit because they were bunched up. With proper spacing, only one gets hit and the others can return fire.

## Poor Radio Discipline

**The Mistake:** Talking too much on comms, unclear transmissions, not listening to your OL.

**Why it's bad:**

- Other teams can't hear important information
- Your OL can't get critical orders through
- Confusion during time-sensitive situations
- Radio clogs up and people miss updates

**How to fix it:**

- Keep transmissions brief and clear
- Listen more than you talk
- Use proper radio procedures (call signs, confirm back, "over/out")
- Don't transmit every little thing—use judgement
- Have your whole message ready before you key the mic

**Example:** You're relaying updates on enemy movement but you're rambling. Your OL tries to send a critical order but can't get a word in. By the time you shut up, the situation has changed.

## Micromanaging Color Teams

**The Mistake:** Telling your color teams exactly where to stand and how to move instead of giving them goals.

**Why it's bad:**

- Teams can't adapt if the situation changes
- They become dependent on you for every decision
- You're distracted micromanaging instead of leading
- They stop thinking and just follow orders blindly

**How to fix it:**

- Give goals and constraints, not position instructions
- Tell them "what" and "why," let them figure out "how"
- Trust them to make good decisions
- Only step in if they're clearly making a bad call

**Example (Wrong):** "Green, move to that tree. Red, set up behind the wall. Blue, watch the corner."

**Example (Right):** "Green, get eyes on that road from the north. Red, establish security on our eastern flank. Blue, watch for movement from the town."

## Not Updating OL on Deviations

**The Mistake:** Your team does something different than ordered, but you don't tell your OL.

**Why it's bad:**

- Your OL makes decisions based on incorrect information
- Other teams don't know where you are
- Coordination breaks down
- Blue-on-blue incidents happen

**How to fix it:**

- Inform OL immediately when you deviate from orders
- Explain why if there's time
- Keep OL in the loop on everything important

**Example:** You're supposed to move to grid 123456 but find a better position 500m away. You move there but don't tell OL. OL plans fire support expecting you at the original position. You almost get hit by your own support.

## Unclear Objectives

**The Mistake:** Your color teams don't fully understand what they're supposed to accomplish.

**Why it's bad:**

- They might do the wrong thing
- They can't adapt when the plan changes
- They're not motivated because they don't know why they're doing this
- Mission fails because of misunderstanding

**How to fix it:**

- Brief the objective clearly before movement
- Explain the bigger picture—why are we doing this?
- Make sure everyone repeats back or confirms they understand
- Update them when things change

**Example:** You tell Red team to hold a building but don't explain why. They evacuate when they see 3 enemy approach, not knowing that support is incoming to trap them. The enemy escapes.

## Not Maintaining Security

**The Mistake:** So focused on the immediate task that you forget to watch for other threats.

**Why it's bad:**

- Ambush from unexpected direction catches you unprepared
- Enemy flanks your position
- You miss enemy movement until they're too close
- Casualties happen because you weren't watching

**How to fix it:**

- Assign sectors of responsibility to each color team
- Check in regularly on what they're seeing
- Maintain perimeter security even during other tasks
- Rotate who's pulling security duty

**Example:** You're clearing a building and stop maintaining watch on the road. Green and Blue miss enemy reinforcements arriving from the road, and you get hit from behind because no one was watching that sector.

## Poor Decision Under Pressure

**The Mistake:** Freezing up, panicking, or making hasty decisions when contact happens.

**Why it's bad:**

- Team looks to you for leadership and you're frozen
- Quick bad decision is worse than no decision
- People die because of confusion and no clear direction

**How to fix it:**

- Remember your priorities (SUPPRESS → TREAT → MOVE)
- Give clear orders even if they're not perfect
- Adjust as you get more information

**Example:** Contact happens. You're not sure where the fire is coming from. Instead of paralysis, you order Blue to suppress right, Green to suppress left, Red to establish rear security. You've bought time to assess and adjust.

## Not Using Terrain

**The Mistake:** Positioning in the open or not using available cover effectively.

**Why it's bad:**

- Your team is exposed and takes casualties from distance
- Enemy has better positioning
- You're forced to move under fire (harder and more dangerous)

**How to fix it:**

- Study the terrain before moving
- Position behind natural cover (trees, rocks, buildings)
- Use high ground when possible
- Brief your color teams on terrain features
- Use bounding and movement techniques to get to better terrain

**Example:** You're moving across open field when ambushed. If you'd taken 5 minutes to route through the valley with trees, the ambush wouldn't have worked and you wouldn't have been pinned.

## Ignoring Equipment Shortages

**The Mistake:** Continuing operations without realizing your team is running low on ammo, medical supplies, or other critical gear.

**Why it's bad:**

- Team can't fight effectively without ammo
- Casualties can't be treated without medical supplies
- You're unprepared when things go wrong
- Mission fails because you don't have specific resources when you need them

**How to fix it:**

- Check equipment status regularly
- Request resupply before you're critically low
- Know what support is available

**Example:** You keep fighting without checking ammo. By the time contact happens again, you're down to 3 mags per person. That's not enough and people die because they can't return fire effectively.

## Not Knowing Your Team

**The Mistake:** You don't know your people, their capabilities, or how to effectively position them.

**Why it's bad:**

- You put a weak shooter in a critical position
- You don't know who to trust under pressure
- Team doesn't know each other well
- Coordination breaks down

**How to fix it:**

- Learn your team members' strengths and capabilities
- Know who's reliable and who needs guidance
- Know who's a good shot, who's the fastest, who's most level-headed
- Position people based on their strengths

**Example:** You put your worst shooter in overwatch when you have a marksman available. You miss the chance to nail enemy at distance because you didn't match the person to the role.

## Over-Trusting or Under-Trusting

**The Mistake:** Giving too much autonomy to inexperienced people or micromanaging experienced ones.

**Why it's bad:**

- New people make mistakes because they're learning
- Experienced people resent being treated like children
- Team morale drops
- Leadership is ineffective

**How to fix it:**

- Match autonomy to experience level
- New people get more guidance, experienced people get more freedom
- Gradually increase autonomy as people prove themselves
- Adjust as you learn who's capable

**Example:** You give a new FTL substitute all the autonomy of an experienced leader. They make a poor tactical call and people get hurt. Or you hover over an experienced member and they get frustrated and make careless mistakes.

## Moving Forward

Every mistake is a teaching moment. After each mission:

- **Reflect on what went wrong**
- **Understand why it happened**
- **Adjust your approach next time**
- **Share lessons with your team**

The FTLs who get better are the ones who learn from mistakes without repeating them. The FTLs who don't improve are the ones who blame others or don't think critically about what happened.

You will make mistakes. Everyone does. The question is whether you learn from them.
