## Setting Up Your Team for Success

Positioning is one of the most important skills an FTL can develop. Good positioning determines what your team can and cannot do, and it directly impacts survival.

## Goal-Based Positioning

Instead of telling your color teams exactly where to stand, give them goals and let them find good positions:

**Bad:** "Green, move to the wall at grid 123456. Position yourself behind the concrete barrier."

**Good:** "Green, I want cover on the eastern approach. Pick a position where you can see that road and nothing can surprise us from that direction."

The difference:

- In the first case, if the position becomes exposed or the situation changes, Green has no clear direction on what to do.
- In the second case, Green understands the *purpose* of their position and can adapt or reposition if needed.

## Security Positioning

A primary concern as an FTL is maintaining 360° awareness and security around your element.

### Basic Principles

- **Depth:** Don't put all your firepower in one spot. Spread your color teams so that if one is engaged, the others can maneuver and suppress the threat.
- **Mutual support:** Teams should be able to cover each other. If one team takes fire, the others can return fire and move to support.
- **Interlocking fields of fire:** Assign each team a direction of responsibility (north, east, south, west) so all angles are covered.
- **Avoid bunching:** Spread out to prevent a single event (ambush, casualty, etc.) from affecting the whole team. A burst of fire should not hit multiple people.

### Common Positions

**Traveling/Movement:**

- Staggered column (one team to the left, one to the right, one slightly back)
- Provides forward security and flanking coverage
- Allow teams to move somewhat independently while maintaining communication

**Defensive/Holding Ground:**

- Triangular or circular formation (teams positioned around a central area)
- Each team covers a sector
- Natural obstacles (buildings, walls, terrain) between teams when possible

**Ambush/Contact:**

- Return fire immediately
- One team suppresses, others maneuver
- Move to covered positions (don't stay in the open where you were hit)

**Clearing Buildings/Areas:**

- One team establishes outer security (watches exits, prevents reinforcements)
- Other team conducts the clear
- Third team maintains overwatch if available

## Assigning Sectors of Responsibility

Each color team should know their area of responsibility:

"Green, you've got security to the north and northeast. I want you focused on that ridge line and the road running along it. Report anything you see moving."

"Blue, you've got the western approaches. Make sure nothing moves through that valley without us knowing."

This is clear, has a purpose, and Green/Blue teams understand what they're responsible for. They don't need detailed position instructions—they understand the goal.

## Using Terrain Effectively

Good terrain provides cover, concealment, and good fields of fire. Help your teams understand terrain:

- **High ground:** Provides better visibility and gives you the advantage in a firefight. Usually worth fighting for.
- **Routes through terrain:** Rivers, tree lines, and valleys provide concealment and cover for movement.
- **Dead space:** Areas an enemy can't see or fire into from their position. Your teams should use these for positioning.
- **Choke points:** Narrow areas (bridges, passes, roads) where a small force can control movement. These are tactically important.

Tell your teams: "Find a position where you can see the main road but the buildings provide cover if we take fire from the north."

Let them find the spot. They might find something better than you would have by looking at a map.

## Spacing and Casualty Prevention

Spacing is critical. Too close together and a single burst of fire wounds multiple people. Too far apart and teams can't support each other or communicate effectively.

**Guidelines:**

- **Buddy pairs:** 3-10 meters apart (visual contact and can hear each other)
- **Within team:** 15-30 meters (spread enough to prevent bunching, but close enough to support each other)
- **Between teams:** 50-100+ meters depending on terrain (far enough that a single contact affects only one team, but close enough to support each other)

When positioning, think about what happens if one team gets hit:

- Can the other teams see where the fire came from?
- Can they move to support?
- Is anyone bunched up together?

## Reacting to Contact

When your team takes fire, positioning determines your options:

1. **Immediate action:** Return fire, move to cover (cover is concealment that also provides protection from fire)
2. **Assess:** Where did the fire come from? How many? What's their position?
3. **Maneuver:** One team suppresses while others move to better positions or flank
4. **Support:** Call for other teams to support or reinforce

Good positioning means you have options. Poor positioning means you're stuck pinned down.

**Example:** Blue is taking fire from a tree line to their east. Blue returns fire immediately and shifts behind a small ridge. Green moves to flank from the south while Red provides cover fire from their position. Blue pops up as Green assaults, suppressing the enemy position. Contact is broken and you maintain the initiative.

## The Role of the FTL in Positioning

- **Assign sectors of responsibility** to your color teams (direction and general area)
- **Identify the goal** (cover the road, establish overwatch, secure a building)
- **Provide constraints** (don't go past the tree line, stay within 100m, keep in line of sight)
- **Trust your teams** to find good positions

Then **step back and let them work.** Trust your teams to find good positions based on the goals you set. If they understand the goal and constraints, they'll find good spots.

## Common Positioning Mistakes

- **Clustering:** Color teams too close together, increases vulnerability to single contacts
- **Predictable patterns:** Using the same formation every time makes you easy to predict and ambush
- **Ignoring terrain:** Missing good positions because you're focused on a map instead of looking at the ground
- **Over-positioning:** Trying to cover too many angles with two color teams, leaves gaps
- **Static positions:** Staying in one spot too long after contact makes you predictable
- **Not coordinating:** Green and Blue not aware of each other's positions, creating overlaps or gaps in coverage
